114 Maintenance Documentation and Logs & Records Fundamentals

Big Picture​

Documentation equals accountability.

Logs and records:

  • Prove airworthiness
  • Provide maintenance history
  • Support investigations
  • Protect legally
  • Drive readiness decisions

If it is not documented, it did not happen.

As an AMDO, you are responsible for ensuring records are accurate, complete, and compliant.

114.1 Discuss the purpose of maintenance documentation​

Maintenance documentation:

  • Records all maintenance actions
  • Tracks aircraft/component history
  • Ensures standardization
  • Provides traceability

Purpose: Ensure safe, legal, and auditable maintenance.

114.2 Discuss the purpose of aircraft logs and records​

Logs and records:

  • Track inspections
  • Record discrepancies
  • Monitor life limits
  • Document repairs and modifications

Purpose: Provide the official history and airworthiness status of the aircraft.

114.3 Discuss responsibilities for logs and records accuracy​

All maintenance personnel must:

  • Enter accurate data
  • Use correct codes
  • Sign actions properly
  • Update promptly

Supervisors must verify correctness.

Inaccurate records can ground aircraft or cause mishaps.

114.4 Discuss the Aircraft Discrepancy Book (ADB)​

ADB:

  • Contains pilot write-ups
  • Lists discrepancies
  • Tracks corrective actions
  • Communicates aircraft condition

Purpose: Daily health record of the aircraft.

Feeds discrepancies into maintenance documentation systems.

114.5 Discuss Maintenance Action Forms (MAFs)​

MAFs document:

  • Discrepancy
  • Troubleshooting
  • Corrective action
  • Parts used
  • Man-hours
  • Inspector sign-offs

Purpose: Permanent record of maintenance performed.

Legal proof of work.

114.6 Discuss the importance of proper sign-offs​

Sign-offs:

  • Certify work completed correctly
  • Establish accountability
  • Confirm inspections performed

Only qualified personnel may sign.

Improper sign-offs invalidate maintenance actions.

114.7 Discuss logbook entries​

Logbooks record:

  • Inspections
  • Repairs
  • Modifications
  • Component changes
  • Flight hours

Purpose: Track aircraft condition and compliance requirements.

Must be legible, accurate, and complete.

114.8 Discuss life-limited component tracking​

Tracks:

  • Hours
  • Cycles
  • Calendar limits

Ensures components are replaced before exceeding limits.

Purpose: Prevent catastrophic failures.

114.9 Discuss configuration documentation​

Tracks:

  • Installed equipment
  • TD compliance
  • Modifications
  • Substitutions

Ensures aircraft matches approved configuration.

Prevents unsafe or unauthorized changes.

114.10 Discuss inspection documentation​

Records:

  • Scheduled inspections
  • Special inspections
  • Conditional inspections
  • Results and findings

Purpose: Prove inspections were completed and aircraft is safe.

114.11 Discuss electronic documentation systems​

Examples:

  • OOMA/NALCOMIS
  • Maintenance databases

Used to:

  • Enter discrepancies
  • Track MAFs
  • Maintain history
  • Generate reports

Purpose: Provide centralized, real-time maintenance records.

114.12 Discuss documentation retention requirements​

Records must be:

  • Maintained per instruction
  • Retained for required timeframes
  • Securely stored
  • Easily retrievable

Purpose: Support audits, investigations, and historical analysis.

114.13 Discuss the purpose of maintenance control logs​

Logs track:

  • Aircraft status
  • Work in progress
  • AWP
  • Downing discrepancies

Purpose: Provide production awareness and accountability.

114.14 Discuss correction of documentation errors​

Errors must be:

  • Lined out with single line
  • Initialed
  • Dated
  • Corrected properly

Never erase or obscure entries.

Maintains integrity of records.

114.15 Discuss documentation for Technical Directive (TD) compliance​

Records:

  • TD applicability
  • Compliance date
  • Method of compliance

Purpose: Prove required safety and configuration changes completed.

Non-compliance may ground aircraft.

114.16 Discuss documentation for cannibalization actions​

Must record:

  • Donor aircraft
  • Receiving aircraft
  • Parts removed/installed
  • Authorization

Purpose: Maintain accountability and configuration accuracy.

114.17 Discuss documentation for removed components​

Includes:

  • Reason for removal
  • Condition
  • Hours/cycles
  • Disposition

Purpose: Support repair tracking and reliability analysis.

114.18 Discuss documentation for support equipment (SE)​

Tracks:

  • Inventory
  • Inspections
  • Calibration
  • Maintenance actions

Ensures SE is safe and serviceable.

Poor SE documentation affects aircraft safety.

114.19 Discuss the importance of legibility and completeness​

Entries must be:

  • Clear
  • Complete
  • Accurate

Incomplete or unreadable records create confusion and risk.

Documentation must be professional and auditable.

114.20 Discuss documentation during shift turnover​

Turnover must include:

  • Aircraft status
  • Outstanding discrepancies
  • Parts status
  • Ongoing work

Ensures continuity between shifts.

Prevents missed tasks or duplication.

114.21 Discuss documentation during deployments or detachments​

Requires:

  • Accurate records transfer
  • Copies of required logs
  • Continuity binders

Ensures maintenance history follows the aircraft.

Prevents loss of critical data.

114.22 Discuss audits of logs and records​

QA audits:

  • Accuracy
  • Completeness
  • Compliance

Purpose: Detect deficiencies early.

Ensures inspection readiness.

114.23 Discuss legal implications of maintenance documentation​

Records:

  • Serve as legal evidence
  • Support investigations
  • Protect personnel and command

False or incomplete entries can result in disciplinary or legal action.

Documentation is legally binding.

114.24 Discuss the relationship between documentation and readiness reporting​

Readiness reports rely on:

  • Accurate status
  • Correct discrepancy coding
  • Proper close-outs

Bad documentation produces false readiness.

Leadership decisions depend on accurate data.

114.25 Discuss the responsibilities of an Aviation Maintenance Officer for logs and records​

The AMDO must:

  • Enforce documentation standards
  • Train personnel
  • Conduct reviews
  • Ensure compliance
  • Correct deficiencies
  • Maintain audit readiness

Bottom line: Accurate documentation ensures safe, legal, and mission capable aircraft.

Quick Oral Board Summary

If asked: "Why are logs and records critical?"

Answer:

"They provide the official history and legal proof that maintenance was completed correctly and the aircraft is airworthy. Without accurate documentation, we cannot ensure safety or readiness."
