117 Aviation Safety Programs and Risk Management Fundamentals

Big Picture​

Safety preserves:

  • Sailors
  • Aircraft
  • Equipment
  • Mission capability

Every mishap:

  • Injures people
  • Costs money
  • Reduces readiness
  • Impacts combat capability

Safety is not separate from maintenance.
Safety is maintenance done correctly.

As an AMDO, you are responsible for embedding safety into every evolution.

117.1 Discuss the purpose of the Naval Aviation Safety Program​

The Naval Aviation Safety Program exists to:

  • Prevent mishaps
  • Preserve lives and assets
  • Identify hazards
  • Share lessons learned
  • Improve readiness

Purpose: Reduce risk while accomplishing the mission.

117.2 Discuss responsibilities of the Command Safety Officer (CSO)​

The CSO:

  • Manages the command safety program
  • Conducts training
  • Tracks hazards
  • Investigates mishaps
  • Advises leadership
  • Ensures reporting compliance

Primary advisor to the CO on safety matters.

117.3 Discuss responsibilities of department/division leadership for safety​

Leaders must:

  • Enforce procedures
  • Supervise evolutions
  • Identify hazards
  • Correct unsafe acts
  • Promote reporting
  • Train personnel

Safety is a leadership responsibility at every level.

117.4 Discuss the concept of Operational Risk Management (ORM)​

ORM is a systematic process to:

  • Identify hazards
  • Assess risk
  • Implement controls
  • Supervise

Purpose: Balance mission accomplishment with safety.

Risk must be deliberate, not accidental.

117.5 Discuss the five steps of ORM​

  1. Identify hazards
  2. Assess hazards
  3. Make risk decisions
  4. Implement controls
  5. Supervise

These steps apply to all maintenance and operational evolutions.

117.6 Discuss hazard identification​

Hazards include:

  • Unsafe acts
  • Unsafe conditions
  • Environmental factors
  • Human factors
  • Equipment issues

Early identification prevents mishaps.

Everyone is responsible for reporting hazards.

117.7 Discuss hazard reporting systems​

Examples:

  • HAZREP
  • Safety reports
  • Near miss reports

Purpose: Capture lessons learned and prevent recurrence fleetwide.

Reporting is non-punitive and prevention focused.

117.8 Discuss mishap classification​

Classes typically based on:

  • Fatalities
  • Injuries
  • Cost of damage

Class A through E

Purpose: Standardize reporting and investigation requirements.

Higher severity requires deeper investigation.

117.9 Discuss maintenance related mishaps​

May include:

  • FOD damage
  • Tool left in aircraft
  • Improper installation
  • Fuel spills
  • Personnel injury

Often caused by:

  • Complacency
  • Poor supervision
  • Procedural non-compliance

Most mishaps are preventable.

117.10 Discuss human factors in maintenance​

Human factors include:

  • Fatigue
  • Complacency
  • Poor communication
  • Inexperience
  • Distraction
  • Time pressure

These increase error probability.

Leaders must recognize and mitigate these risks.

117.11 Discuss fatigue management​

Fatigue reduces:

  • Attention
  • Judgment
  • Reaction time

Controls include:

  • Proper scheduling
  • Rest periods
  • Watch rotation
  • Supervisory awareness

Fatigued personnel make mistakes.

117.12 Discuss PPE requirements​

PPE includes:

  • Hearing protection
  • Eye protection
  • Cranials
  • Gloves
  • Steel toe boots
  • Respiratory protection

Purpose: Reduce injury severity.

No PPE equals unnecessary risk.

117.13 Discuss hazardous energy control (Tag-Out/Lock-Out)​

Prevents:

  • Electrical shock
  • Mechanical movement
  • Pressurized system release

Requires:

  • Securing energy source
  • Tagging/locking
  • Verification

Protects personnel during maintenance.

117.14 Discuss fire prevention procedures​

Includes:

  • Proper storage of flammables
  • No ignition sources
  • Fire watches
  • Housekeeping
  • Proper grounding during fueling

Purpose: Prevent fires before they start.

Prevention is better than response.

117.15 Discuss emergency response procedures​

Personnel must know actions for:

  • Fire
  • Fuel spill
  • Aircraft crash
  • Medical emergency
  • Severe weather

Immediate reporting and response limits damage and saves lives.

117.16 Discuss foreign object damage (FOD) hazards​

FOD can:

  • Damage engines
  • Injure personnel
  • Ground aircraft

Prevention includes:

  • Walkdowns
  • Tool control
  • Housekeeping
  • Covers

FOD prevention is everyone’s responsibility.

117.17 Discuss tool and equipment safety​

Requires:

  • Proper use
  • Inspections
  • Accountability
  • Serviceability checks

Defective or missing tools create hazards.

Tool discipline equals aircraft safety.

117.18 Discuss hazardous material (HAZMAT) safety​

Includes:

  • Proper labeling
  • PPE use
  • Ventilation
  • Storage
  • Disposal procedures

Protects personnel and environment.

Improper handling creates health and fire risks.

117.19 Discuss safety training requirements​

Training includes:

  • ORM
  • Firefighting
  • HAZMAT
  • PPE
  • Emergency procedures

Ensures personnel know how to recognize and mitigate hazards.

Training prevents accidents.

117.20 Discuss safety inspections​

Safety inspections:

  • Identify unsafe conditions
  • Check compliance
  • Verify controls

Performed regularly by safety and leadership.

Purpose: Correct hazards before mishaps occur.

117.21 Discuss the importance of near-miss reporting​

Near misses:

  • Reveal hidden hazards
  • Provide early warning
  • Prevent future mishaps

Reporting them allows corrective action without injury or damage.

Every near miss is a lesson.

117.22 Discuss communication in safety​

Requires:

  • Clear briefs
  • Proper hand signals
  • Positive control
  • Immediate hazard reporting

Poor communication leads to preventable mishaps.

Safety depends on clear communication.

117.23 Discuss supervisory responsibilities during hazardous evolutions​

Supervisors must:

  • Brief personnel
  • Verify qualifications
  • Monitor execution
  • Stop unsafe acts
  • Enforce procedures

Active supervision reduces risk.

117.24 Discuss the relationship between safety and readiness​

Good safety:

  • Fewer injuries
  • Less downtime
  • Reduced damage
  • Higher aircraft availability

Safety directly improves readiness.

Unsafe operations reduce combat capability.

117.25 Discuss the responsibilities of an Aviation Maintenance Officer for safety programs​

The AMDO must:

  • Enforce ORM
  • Ensure training
  • Supervise hazardous evolutions
  • Promote reporting culture
  • Correct unsafe practices
  • Integrate safety into daily operations

Bottom line: Safe maintenance produces ready aircraft.

Quick Oral Board Summary

If asked: "How does safety affect readiness?"

Answer:

"Every mishap costs time, money, and aircraft availability. By preventing hazards and enforcing safe procedures, we protect Sailors and keep aircraft mission capable."
