101 Safety Fundamentals

101.1 Discuss the concept of Operational Risk Management (ORM)​

Operational Risk Management is a systematic decision-making process used to identify hazards, assess risk, and implement controls to reduce risk to an acceptable level while still accomplishing the mission.
It balances mission accomplishment with personnel and equipment safety and is integrated into all planning and execution phases of naval aviation operations.

101.2 Explain the following as they apply to ORM​

a. Identifying hazards​

Recognizing conditions or actions that can cause injury, damage, or mission degradation. Examples include FOD, fatigue, weather, or improper procedures.

b. Assessing hazards​

Determining the probability and severity of each hazard to assign a risk level (low to extremely high).

c. Making risk decisions​

Deciding whether the risk is acceptable, or if controls or supervision are required before proceeding.

d. Implementing controls​

Putting mitigation measures in place such as PPE, training, procedures, barriers, or supervision.

e. Supervising​

Ensuring controls remain effective and adjusting as conditions change through continuous oversight and feedback.

101.3 Discuss the purpose and goal of the Navy Safety Management System (SMS)​

The Navy Safety Management System provides a standardized framework to manage risk across all operations.
Its goal is to prevent mishaps, protect personnel and equipment, and preserve combat readiness by integrating safety into leadership, training, and daily operations.

101.4 Discuss the purpose of the Naval Aviation Safety Management System​

It specifically applies SMS principles to aviation operations.
It reduces aviation mishaps through reporting, investigation, trend analysis, training, and leadership accountability to protect aircrew, maintainers, and aircraft.

101.5 Identify the types and circumstances under which safety reports are prepared​

Safety reports are submitted for:

  • Aircraft mishaps
  • Ground mishaps
  • Near misses
  • Hazardous conditions
  • Material failures
  • HAZREPs and ASAPs

They are used to identify root causes, share lessons learned, and prevent recurrence.

101.6 Discuss how the following causal factors contribute to aviation mishaps​

a. Human​

Fatigue, complacency, poor communication, lack of training, procedural violations, or poor supervision.

b. Material​

Equipment failure, improper maintenance, design defects, or worn components.

101.7 Define aircraft mishap severity classes​

Class A​

Fatality, permanent disability, or damage ≥ $2.5M.

Class B​

Hospitalization or damage between ~$600K and $2.5M.

Class C​

Lost workdays or moderate damage.

Class D​

Minor injuries or minimal damage.

Class E​

Near miss or hazard with no injury/damage but high potential.

101.8 Define mishap subcategories​

a. Flight Mishap (FM)​

Occurs during flight or intent for flight.

b. Flight Related Mishap (FRM)​

Supports flight operations but not airborne (taxi, towing, maintenance).

c. Aircraft Ground Mishap (AGM)​

Occurs while aircraft is stationary or not related to flight ops.

101.9 Discuss the composition and purpose of the Aircraft Mishap Board​

Composed of safety officers, maintenance reps, medical, and SMEs.
Purpose is to investigate root causes, recommend corrective actions, and prevent future mishaps.
It is not punitive, it is prevention focused.

101.10 Discuss safety precautions​

a. Confined spaces​

Gas testing, ventilation, permits, attendants, PPE.

b. Electrical equipment​

Tag-out, de-energize, insulated tools, PPE.

c. High noise​

Double hearing protection, time limits.

d. Flammables​

Ventilation, grounding, no ignition sources.

e. Compressed gases​

Secure bottles, proper regulators, protective caps.

f. Heavy lifting​

Use proper technique, team lifts, mechanical aids.

g. RADHAZ​

Observe safe distances, secure transmitters, follow posted limits.

h. Asbestos​

Do not disturb, use certified removal, respiratory protection.

101.11 Safety precautions during a fire​

  • Sound alarm
  • Report location
  • Secure power/fuel
  • Fight fire if trained
  • Evacuate when required
  • Muster and account for personnel

101.12 Severe weather precautions​

  • Secure aircraft/equipment
  • Tie-downs and chocks
  • Close doors/hatches
  • Stage emergency gear
  • Protect personnel
  • Follow heavy weather bills

101.13 First aid procedures​

a. Remove from energized circuit​

Secure power first or use nonconductive tool.

b. Shock​

Lay flat, keep warm, monitor airway.

c. Burns​

Cool with water, cover sterile dressing.

d. Bleeding​

Direct pressure, elevate, tourniquet if required.

e. Heat exhaustion​

Cool area, hydrate, rest.

f. Frostbite​

Warm slowly, protect tissue, no rubbing.

101.14 Discuss the following​

a. Sight conservation​

Eye protection and hazard controls.

b. Battery safety​

Avoid acid exposure and sparks.

c. Foot protection​

Steel-toe or flight deck boots.

d. Head protection​

Helmets or cranials.

e. Toxic protection​

Respirators and ventilation.

f. Hazardous labels​

Communicate chemical hazards.

g. Electromagnetic hazards​

Limit exposure around transmitters.

h. Hearing conservation​

Earplugs/muffs and monitoring.

101.15 Services provided by Naval Safety Command​

  • Safety magazines and publications
  • ALSAFE messages
  • Safety surveys
  • Website resources
  • Maintenance checklists
  • Engine cost data
  • Command climate assessments
  • Maintenance climate assessments

101.16 Define maintenance publication terms​

Warning​

Risk of death or serious injury.

Caution​

Risk of equipment damage or minor injury.

Note​

Helpful information.

Must/Will​

Mandatory.

Should​

Recommended.

May​

Optional/authorized.

101.17 Role of Industrial Hygienist​

Identifies and controls workplace hazards such as noise, chemicals, and air quality.
Conducts exposure monitoring and recommends PPE and engineering controls.

101.18 Flight deck/line safety precautions​

Includes:

  • Proper cranial and PPE
  • Avoid rotor arcs and jet blast
  • FOD control
  • Safe aircraft movement
  • Fueling precautions
  • Leak response
  • Weapons safety
  • Night visibility procedures
  • Emergency landing awareness

101.19 Role of Aircraft Maintenance Coordinator (AMC) and Flight Line Coordinator (FLC) in preventing mishaps​

They coordinate aircraft movement, maintenance, and flight line operations.
They ensure safe sequencing, communication, and deconfliction to prevent personnel or aircraft incidents.
