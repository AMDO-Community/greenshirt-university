105 Administration Fundamentals

Big Picture​

Administration in maintenance is about:

  • Accountability
  • Traceability
  • Compliance
  • Standardization

If it isn’t documented, it didn’t happen.

Admin programs protect:

  • Safety
  • Legal compliance
  • Readiness
  • Funding
  • Inspections

As a leader, you are responsible for ensuring programs are followed, records are accurate, and maintenance actions are auditable.

105.1 Discuss the purpose of maintenance administration​

Maintenance administration ensures:

  • Work is properly documented
  • Maintenance is standardized
  • Regulations are followed
  • Historical data is preserved
  • Readiness can be measured

It provides legal and technical proof that maintenance was performed correctly.

Without admin control, aircraft safety and airworthiness cannot be verified.

105.2 Discuss the purpose of logs and records​

Logs and records:

  • Track aircraft/component history
  • Record inspections and maintenance
  • Track life limits
  • Document discrepancies
  • Provide trend analysis

They ensure: Airworthiness, safety, and compliance with NAMP.

They also protect the command legally during investigations or mishaps.

105.3 Discuss technical publications (pubs)​

Technical publications:

  • Provide approved procedures
  • Standardize maintenance
  • Prevent unsafe practices

Examples:

  • MIMs
  • IPBs
  • NATEC pubs
  • NATOPS
  • TDs

Only current, authorized publications may be used.

Using outdated pubs can cause unsafe or illegal maintenance.

105.4 Discuss the purpose of the Central Technical Publications Library (CTPL)​

CTPL:

  • Maintains official pubs
  • Controls distribution
  • Ensures currency
  • Tracks revisions
  • Removes obsolete material

Purpose: Guarantee maintainers always use the latest authorized procedures.

It is the command’s single source of truth for maintenance instructions.

105.5 Discuss configuration management​

Configuration management ensures:

  • Aircraft match approved design
  • Modifications are tracked
  • Correct parts are installed
  • Records reflect actual configuration

Includes:

  • TD compliance
  • Part substitutions
  • Component tracking

Goal: No unauthorized or undocumented changes.

105.6 Discuss Technical Directives (TDs)​

TDs are official instructions to:

  • Modify
  • Inspect
  • Repair
  • Replace
  • Restrict

Types include:

  • Safety
  • Immediate action
  • Routine

Compliance may be mandatory or conditional.

TDs ensure fleet standardization and safety.

105.7 Discuss the purpose of the Aircraft Discrepancy Book (ADB)​

ADB:

  • Tracks aircraft discrepancies
  • Documents downing issues
  • Records corrective actions

Provides:

  • Communication between aircrew and maintenance
  • Legal record of aircraft condition

It is the daily health record of the aircraft.

105.8 Discuss the Maintenance Action Form (MAF)​

MAF documents:

  • Discrepancy
  • Troubleshooting
  • Corrective action
  • Parts used
  • Man-hours
  • Sign-offs

Purpose: Permanent record of maintenance performed.

Used for:

  • Trend analysis
  • Cost tracking
  • QA review
  • Readiness metrics

105.9 Discuss the purpose of documentation accuracy​

Accurate documentation ensures:

  • Safety
  • Accountability
  • Auditability
  • Legal protection
  • Correct readiness reporting

Poor documentation can:

  • Ground aircraft
  • Cause inspections to fail
  • Lead to mishaps

Standard: Clear, complete, and legible.

105.10 Discuss the Quality Assurance (QA) administrative role​

QA:

  • Audits maintenance programs
  • Monitors compliance
  • Tracks trends
  • Conducts inspections
  • Manages CDIs/QARs

Purpose: Ensure maintenance meets standards and prevent repeat errors.

QA is the command’s internal safety net.

105.11 Discuss tool control program administration​

Tool control ensures:

  • All tools accounted for
  • No tools left in aircraft
  • Prevents FOD

Includes:

  • Shadowing
  • Tool inventories
  • Tool issue logs

Missing tools are treated as potential flight safety hazards.

105.12 Discuss maintenance program binders/SOPs​

Program binders contain:

  • Local instructions
  • Procedures
  • Responsibilities
  • References

They standardize how the command executes programs.

Purpose: Ensure continuity during personnel turnover and inspections.

105.13 Discuss training documentation​

Training documentation tracks:

  • Qualifications
  • Certifications
  • Schools
  • PQS completion

Systems:

  • FLTMPS
  • ASM

Purpose: Ensure only qualified personnel perform maintenance.

If not documented, the qualification does not exist.

105.14 Discuss the importance of audits and inspections​

Audits:

  • Verify compliance
  • Identify weaknesses
  • Improve processes

Examples:

  • QA audits
  • Programs audits
  • TYCOM inspections
  • AMI

Goal: Continuous improvement and readiness.

Inspections should validate good programs, not surprise you.

105.15 Discuss correspondence and message traffic​

Official communication methods:

  • Naval messages
  • Instructions
  • Letters
  • Memos

Used for:

  • Policy changes
  • Requirements
  • Reporting

Must follow proper format and routing.

Provides formal, traceable communication.

105.16 Discuss records retention requirements​

Maintenance records must be:

  • Retained per instruction
  • Securely stored
  • Easily retrievable

Some records:

  • Short term
  • Life of component
  • Permanent

Purpose: Support investigations, audits, and historical tracking.

105.17 Discuss supply and maintenance coordination​

Admin coordination ensures:

  • Parts ordered correctly
  • Turn-in documented
  • Cannibalizations tracked
  • Awaiting parts managed

Proper paperwork ensures: Material readiness and accurate logistics tracking.

Maintenance and supply must work as one team.

105.18 Discuss the impact of administration on readiness​

Good admin:

  • Faster troubleshooting
  • Better trend analysis
  • Fewer repeat discrepancies
  • Smooth inspections

Poor admin:

  • Lost parts
  • Missed inspections
  • Unsafe aircraft
  • Failed audits

Admin is directly tied to aircraft availability.

Quick Oral Board Summary

If asked: "Why does administration matter to an AMDO?"

Answer:

"Because administration creates accountability and traceability. It ensures maintenance is safe, standardized, and legally defensible. Without proper documentation, we cannot prove airworthiness or readiness, which directly impacts mission success."
