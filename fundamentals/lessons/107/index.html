107 Afloat Aviation Operations Fundamentals

Big Picture​

Afloat aviation operations place aircraft, fuel, ordnance, and personnel in a confined shipboard environment.
Risk is higher than ashore, therefore strict procedures, positive control, and trained watchstanders are required to prevent catastrophic mishaps.

As a maintenance leader, you must understand deck organization, fueling, aircraft movement, crash response, and supervisory responsibilities.

107.1 Discuss flight and hangar deck preparation requirements for the following:​

a. UNREP​

Secure unnecessary flight ops, conduct FOD walkdown, strike/secure aircraft, stage firefighting teams, and clear transfer stations to safely conduct replenishment.

b. CONREP​

Clear rigging areas, secure aircraft near stations, establish safety observers, and maintain safe equipment routing.

c. VERTREP​

Establish clear landing zones, remove loose gear, post LSEs, secure lightweight equipment from rotor wash.

d. Alert posture​

Aircraft fueled/configured, launch paths clear, crash crews manned, support equipment staged for rapid response.

e. Heavy weather​

Hangar or double chain aircraft, secure equipment, close doors, suspend nonessential ops, continuous monitoring.

f. Pier side​

Chock/chain aircraft, grounding wires installed, fire equipment available, controlled taxi routes.

g. Anchored​

Maintain alert readiness, secure aircraft for wind/swell, watchstanders posted.

h. Ammunition on/offload​

Explosive safety arcs established, HERO controls in place, firefighting staged, qualified personnel only.

107.2 Discuss Integrity Watch responsibilities​

Integrity Watch maintains aircraft and space safety when operations are secured.

Responsibilities include:

  • Check tiedowns/chains
  • Monitor leaks/spills
  • FOD control
  • Fire hazard checks
  • Control unauthorized access
  • Report discrepancies immediately

Purpose is to preserve readiness and prevent overnight damage or mishaps.

107.3 Discuss aviation fueling system personnel responsibilities and interaction​

Fueling is high risk due to fire and explosion hazards.

  • Air Boss authorizes evolution
  • Aviation Fuels Officer manages program
  • Fuel crew supervisor directs operation
  • Nozzleman connects and monitors
  • Plane Captain prepares aircraft
  • Crash crew stands by

Grounding/bonding required and anyone may call STOP FUELING.

107.4 Discuss flight deck organization and control​

Flight deck operations are controlled by:

  • Air Boss
  • Handler
  • PriFly

They coordinate launch, recovery, and deck spotting to maintain safe and efficient operations.

Only one controlling authority directs aircraft movement.

107.5 Discuss required flight deck firefighting and safety equipment​

Includes:

  • AFFF stations
  • PKP extinguishers
  • Fire bottles
  • Spill kits
  • Crash crane
  • Rescue equipment

Personnel must know locations and operation.

Immediate response capability is mandatory.

107.6 Discuss aircraft movement procedures on the flight deck​

Aircraft are moved using:

  • Directors
  • Standard hand signals
  • Wing walkers
  • Chocks/chains
  • Safe taxi paths

Hazards include jet blast, prop/rotor arcs, and intakes.

Positive control prevents personnel injury and aircraft damage.

107.7 Discuss FOD prevention and control procedures​

FOD prevention includes:

  • Walkdowns
  • Tool accountability
  • Housekeeping
  • Immediate debris removal
  • Intake/exhaust covers

Purpose is to prevent engine damage and mishaps.

Clean deck equals safe deck.

107.8 Discuss hangar bay operations and precautions​

Hangar operations require:

  • Fire boundary integrity
  • Ventilation
  • Hot work controls
  • Equipment staging
  • Clear egress routes

Fuel vapors and confined space hazards increase fire risk.

Strict safety compliance required.

107.9 Discuss aircraft spotting and securing procedures​

Aircraft spotting considers:

  • Launch sequence
  • Wind direction
  • Elevator access
  • Fire lanes

Securing uses:

  • Chains
  • Chocks
  • Tie-downs

Heavy weather requires additional restraints.

107.10 Discuss aircraft elevator operations​

Elevators move aircraft between decks.

Safety requirements:

  • Weight limits observed
  • Area clear of personnel
  • Positive communications
  • Locks engaged

Movement only with authorized control.

107.11 Discuss flight deck communications methods​

Includes:

  • Sound powered phones
  • Radios
  • Hand signals
  • Status boards

Clear communication prevents confusion and mishaps.

Always verify, never assume.

107.12 Discuss crash and salvage team responsibilities​

Crash and salvage provides:

  • Firefighting
  • Rescue
  • Aircraft recovery
  • Spill containment

They are manned during high risk evolutions and must respond immediately.

107.13 Discuss ordnance safety afloat​

Controls include:

  • Explosive arcs
  • HERO restrictions
  • Segregated storage
  • Certified personnel only
  • Strict accountability

Weapons increase hazard level and require procedural discipline.

107.14 Discuss required personnel protective equipment (PPE)​

Required PPE includes:

  • Cranial
  • Goggles
  • Hearing protection
  • Float coat
  • Gloves
  • Steel toe boots

PPE reduces severity of injuries in hazardous deck environments.

107.15 Discuss emergency procedures for flight/hangar decks​

Personnel must know actions for:

  • Fire
  • Fuel spill
  • Aircraft crash
  • Hung ordnance
  • Severe weather
  • Man overboard

Immediate reporting and chain of command notification required.

Rapid response limits damage and saves lives.

107.16 Discuss the responsibilities of an Aviation Maintenance Officer during afloat operations​

Responsibilities include:

  • Enforcing NATOPS compliance
  • Ensuring qualified personnel
  • Supervising fueling/maintenance safely
  • Coordinating with Air Department
  • Managing risk and readiness
  • Protecting aircraft and Sailors

Bottom line: Safe deck operations directly determine mission capability.

Quick Oral Board Summary

If asked: "What is your role in afloat aviation operations?"

Answer:

"My role is to ensure maintenance and flight deck evolutions are conducted safely, by qualified personnel, under positive control, and in accordance with NATOPS. Safe operations preserve aircraft, Sailors, and mission readiness."
